Interactive Physics Experiments

Explore fundamental physics concepts through hands-on simulations. Adjust the controls and observe the results in real time.

Experiment 1: Force and Pressure

What you will learn: Pressure is the force applied per unit area. When the same force acts on a smaller area, the pressure increases — this is why a nail tip pierces easily while a flat palm does not.
Pressure = 2.00 N/cm²

Experiment 2: Friction

What you will learn: Friction is a force that opposes motion between surfaces in contact. Its magnitude depends on the nature of the surfaces (coefficient of friction) and the weight of the object. An object moves only when the applied force exceeds friction.
Friction = 20.00 N | Object stays still

Experiment 3: Sound Waves

What you will learn: Sound travels as longitudinal waves. Here we visualise it as a transverse sine wave. Frequency determines pitch (how many waves per second), and amplitude determines loudness (wave height). Wavelength is the distance between consecutive crests.
Frequency: 200 Hz | Amplitude: 60

Experiment 4: Light — Reflection

What you will learn: The law of reflection states that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. Flat mirrors reflect light symmetrically. Concave mirrors converge light, while convex mirrors diverge it.
θi = 45° | θr = 45° | Mirror: Flat

Experiment 5: Light — Refraction

What you will learn: When light passes from one medium to another (e.g., air to glass), it changes speed and bends. Snell’s Law — n₁ sin(θi) = n₂ sin(θr) — governs the relationship between the angles and the refractive indices of the two media.
θi = 30° | θr = 19.47° | Medium: Glass (n=1.5)

Experiment 6: Gravity & Free Fall

What you will learn: All objects fall at the same rate regardless of their mass (ignoring air resistance). Galileo demonstrated this from the Leaning Tower of Pisa. The acceleration due to gravity on Earth is 9.8 m/s², on the Moon it is 1.6 m/s², and on Jupiter it is 24.8 m/s². Distance fallen: s = ½gt².
Click "Drop Objects" to start. Both objects fall at the same rate!

Experiment 7: Pulley System

What you will learn: Pulleys reduce the effort needed to lift a load. A single fixed pulley changes the direction of force (MA=1). A double pulley gives MA=2, and a triple pulley gives MA=3. Mechanical Advantage = Load / Effort.
Load = 100 N | Effort = 100 N | Mechanical Advantage = 1

Experiment 8: Lever & Fulcrum

What you will learn: A lever is a rigid bar that rotates about a fixed point called the fulcrum. The principle of moments states: For balance, clockwise moment = anticlockwise moment. Moment = Force × Distance from fulcrum.
Left Moment = 90 Nm | Right Moment = 90 Nm | Balanced!

Experiment 9: Density & Floating

What you will learn: An object floats if its density is less than the liquid's density, and sinks if greater. Water has a density of 1.0 g/cm³ and oil has 0.8 g/cm³. Wood (0.6) floats on both, iron (7.8) sinks in both, and ice (0.9) floats on water but sinks in oil.
Select an object and liquid, then click "Drop Object".

Experiment 10: Spring & Hooke’s Law

What you will learn: Hooke’s Law states that the extension of a spring is directly proportional to the force applied, as long as the elastic limit is not exceeded. Formula: F = k × x, where k is the spring constant and x is the extension.
Force = 19.6 N | Extension = 0.98 m | F = kx

Experiment 11: Pendulum Motion

What you will learn: A simple pendulum swings back and forth with a period that depends only on its length and the acceleration due to gravity, not on the mass. The formula is T = 2π√(L/g), where T is the time period, L is the length, and g is gravity.
T = 2.01 s | Length = 1.0 m | g = 9.8 m/s²

Experiment 12: Wave Interference

What you will learn: When two waves meet, they interfere. If the crests align (in phase), the amplitude increases — this is constructive interference. If a crest meets a trough (out of phase), they cancel out — this is destructive interference.
Phase Diff = 0° | Constructive Interference

Experiment 13: Electric Circuit

What you will learn: An electric circuit needs a complete path for current to flow. Ohm’s Law: V = I × R. More batteries increase voltage, more resistors decrease current. Bulb brightness depends on current flowing through it.
V = 1.5 V | I = 0.15 A | R = 10 Ω | Switch: ON

Experiment 14: Magnetic Field Lines

What you will learn: A magnet has two poles: North (N) and South (S). Magnetic field lines travel from the North pole to the South pole outside the magnet. Iron filings align along these field lines, revealing the invisible magnetic field pattern.
Single bar magnet | Field lines: N to S outside the magnet

Experiment 15: Heat Conduction

What you will learn: Heat travels through solids by conduction. Metals like copper conduct heat quickly, iron conducts moderately, and wood is a poor conductor (insulator). The temperature decreases along the rod from the heated end.
Copper | Source: 200°C | Good conductor - heat spreads quickly

Experiment 16: Convex & Concave Lens

What you will learn: A convex lens converges light rays and can form real or virtual images depending on the object distance. A concave lens always diverges light and forms virtual, erect, diminished images. Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f. Magnification = v/u.
Convex Lens | u = 200 px | v = 133 px | Magnification = 0.67x | Real, Inverted

Experiment 17: Prism & Dispersion

What you will learn: White light is made up of seven colours (VIBGYOR). When it passes through a prism, each colour bends by a different amount because each has a different wavelength. Violet bends the most and red the least. This splitting of light is called dispersion.
Prism Angle = 60° | White light disperses into VIBGYOR spectrum

Experiment 18: Projectile Motion

What you will learn: When an object is launched at an angle, it follows a parabolic path. The horizontal motion is uniform while the vertical motion is accelerated by gravity. Range = (v² sin2θ)/g. Maximum height = (v² sin²θ)/(2g). Maximum range occurs at 45°.
Range = 255.1 m | Max Height = 63.8 m | Time of Flight = 7.21 s

Experiment 19: Archimedes’ Principle

What you will learn: When an object is immersed in a fluid, it experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces. Buoyant Force = ρ_fluid × V_submerged × g. The apparent weight loss of the object equals the buoyant force.
Weight = 2.94 N | Buoyant Force = 0.98 N | Apparent Weight = 1.96 N

Experiment 20: Speed of Sound

What you will learn: Sound travels at different speeds through different media. In air it is about 343 m/s, in water about 1480 m/s, and in steel about 5960 m/s. Sound travels fastest through solids and slowest through gases. Thunder-lightning delay helps calculate distance: Distance = Speed × Time.
Speed = 343 m/s (Air) | Thunder delay = 3 s | Lightning distance = 1029 m (1.03 km)

Experiment 21: Electromagnetic Induction

What you will learn: When a magnet moves through a coil, it changes the magnetic flux and induces an electric current (Faraday’s Law). The faster the magnet moves, the greater the induced current. Reversing the direction reverses the current.
Move the magnet through the coil to induce current.

Experiment 22: Thermal Expansion

What you will learn: When materials are heated, their particles vibrate more and move apart, causing the material to expand. Solids expand the least, liquids expand more, and gases expand the most. This is why railway tracks have gaps and bridges have expansion joints.
Solid at 25°C | Expansion: minimal

Experiment 23: Pascal’s Law (Hydraulic Press)

What you will learn: Pascal’s Law states that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally in all directions. In a hydraulic press, a small force on a small piston creates a large force on a large piston. F₂/F₁ = A₂/A₁.
Input: 20 N on 10 cm² | Output: 200 N on 100 cm² | Force Multiplied 10x

Experiment 24: Bernoulli’s Principle

What you will learn: Bernoulli’s Principle states that as the speed of a fluid increases, its pressure decreases. This is why airplane wings generate lift: air moves faster over the curved top surface, creating lower pressure above and higher pressure below, producing an upward force.
Airspeed = 50 m/s | Lift Force proportional to speed²

Experiment 25: Moment of Force (Torque)

What you will learn: The moment of a force (torque) is the turning effect of a force about a pivot. Moment = Force × Perpendicular distance from pivot. For an object to be in rotational equilibrium, the sum of clockwise moments must equal the sum of anticlockwise moments.
CW Moment = 60 Nm | ACW Moment = 60 Nm | Balanced!
